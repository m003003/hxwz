【人物专访】《南京浩劫》作者张纯如

〖编者按：张纯如（Ｉｒｉｓ　Ｃｈａｎｇ）是精装本畅销书《被遗忘的大屠杀：１９３７南京浩劫》（英文书名：Ｔｈｅ　Ｒａｐｅ　ｏｆ　Ｎａｎｋｉｎｇ。中文书名根据该书中文版，下简称为《南京浩劫》）一书作者，现年２９岁。本次采访是在她为该书巡回推销的途中进行的。双方用英文交谈，原文全篇刊载于ＣＮＤ－Ｇｌｏｂａｌ今年第３０期（二月二十四日）上。翻译工作由《华夏文摘》编辑部集体完成。

时间：一九九八年二月三日

地点：美国华盛顿州西雅图市Ａｌｅｘｉｓ旅馆

记者：余子坚（通过电话），下称ＣＮＤ

ＣＮＤ：Ｉｒｉｓ，谢谢你接受这次采访。我知道一个小时以后你又要去参加一次签名售书活动。

张纯如：是的，但是没有关系。我们试试看可以谈多久。

ＣＮＤ：好的。请你谈一下这本书的销售情况好吗？

张纯如：销售情况非常好。它将在《纽约时报》畅销书排行榜（二月八日）上排名第十一。〖编注：在四月五日的排行榜上这本书居第十四位，是其连续第十周名列《纽约时报》畅销书排行榜。〗

ＣＮＤ：好极了。

张纯如：实际上这一周（二月一日）它的排名是第十四。

ＣＮＤ：名次还在上升。签名售书的情况怎么样？

张纯如：也很好。通常每次签名售书都可以看到一百多人参加。

ＣＮＤ：你当初预想到这本书会造成这么大的轰动吗？

张纯如：没有。我起初想这本书可能会在学术界得到一些好评，但不曾想它会成为全国畅销书。

ＣＮＤ：是什么原因使它造成这么强烈的反响呢？

张纯如：我想在美国的中国人等这样一本书已经等了六十年，这恐怕是它能这么畅销的原因吧。此外，这本书首次披露的一些屠杀事件使很多非中国人、非亚洲人也感到震惊。

ＣＮＤ：参加签名售书的主要是中国人吗？

张纯如：不一定。有时参加的白人比亚洲人还多。

ＣＮＤ：这大概也是你期望的？

张纯如：我希望所有种族的人都读这本书。

ＣＮＤ：据你所知这本书会被翻译成日文吗？

张纯如：我现在还不知道。我听说有人想把它翻成日文，但我不能肯定这件事。

ＣＮＤ：那么其它的语言呢？

张纯如：台北的天下文化出版公司已经以我的中文名字――张纯如出版了这本书。〖编注：书名为《被遗忘的大屠杀：１９３７南京浩劫》。〗

ＣＮＤ：我们谈一下你为写这本书所做的研究吧。你起初为什么要写这本书？

张纯如：我要写这本书是因为我不想看到这个事件被人们淡忘，从历史中消失。

ＣＮＤ：你写这本书之前还没有一本英文的关于南京大屠杀的书。

张纯如：是的，那时还没有。我小时候从我父母那里听说了这个事件。一九九四年十二月我参加了纪念南京大屠杀的库柏蒂诺会议，听说还没有一本以此为题材的书，我于是想改变这种状况。〖编注：库柏蒂诺位于北加州，在那里举行的南京大屠杀死难者悼念会由“亚洲第二次世界大战史实维护会全球联盟”主办。〗

ＣＮＤ：那么你的研究主要是基于原始材料了？

张纯如：是的。原始文献、未公开的材料。我找到数千页的文献，有中文、日文、德文和英文，加上一些从未出版的日记、笔记、信函、政府报告。我还查阅了东京战犯审判记录稿。

ＣＮＤ：这些材料容易得到吗？

张纯如：不是很难。当然现在我已经很精于挖掘材料了。在我写第一本书时〖编注：《蚕丝》，一九九六年出版，“中国导弹之父”钱学森的传记〗，挖掘材料非常困难。同那本书比，这本《南京浩劫》相当容易写，各项工作都比较简单。再有，南京大屠杀发生在三十年代，现在很多文献已经被存档并被美国政府解密，确实比写第一本书（讲的是五十年代的事）要容易。我找到了很多可以利用的资料。

ＣＮＤ：这大概更使你觉得奇怪为什么还没有一本关于南京大屠杀的英文书？

张纯如：说得是！但从另一方面讲，很多人不知道到什么地方去找这些材料。我是一个好侦探，我知道到哪里去找。比如说，我没费多大力气就找到了拉贝的后裔。很多其他学者找了几十年也不得法。

ＣＮＤ：也许这本书就应该由你来写。

张纯如：我是用了很大耐心的。动笔之前我就尽力找到关于这次屠杀的每一细节的材料。我试图收集尽量多的从不同立足点记录的观点。我认为挖掘尽量多的材料很重要，在此基础上消化这些材料，在众多的资料上建立起事情的构架。

ＣＮＤ：你共收集了多少材料？

张纯如：几千页，装了几个文件柜。

ＣＮＤ：工作量真是大得惊人。而且你也去了中国和日本？

张纯如：我去了中国。我没有去日本，因为我主要是通过书信和日本二战老兵联系。我去过南京。

ＣＮＤ：什么时候去的？

张纯如：一九九五年夏天。

ＣＮＤ：夏天？那可不是去南京的好时候。南京的夏天出了名的热。

张纯如：确实如此！

ＣＮＤ：你采访受害者了吗？

张纯如：采访了，而且我给十来个提供证词的受害者摄了像――有在台湾的，也有在南京的。在南京我大概给十个幸存者摄了像。

ＣＮＤ：哦，是这样。那么你是独自去的吗？就你自己一个人？

张纯如：是的。

ＣＮＤ：对于在美国长大的你来说，在中国旅行还算适应吗？

张纯如：当时我只觉得有一项使命需要我去完成，所以我想谈不上适应还是不适应。

ＣＮＤ：你的目标非常明确。

张纯如：我的目标非常明确，我也没有时间去想我是不是适应那里的文化。不过我喜欢我在那儿遇到的人们。我喜欢那些帮助我的学者和历史学家们，而且我非常喜爱南京这座城市。我不是指南京的天气――我常常会身体不太舒服――但在其它方面我都没有任何问题。我懂中文而且能用普通话与人交谈，所以与当地人谈话并了解他们的生活方式其实满有意思。与我交谈的每一个学者都讲了关于他们自己生活的非常精彩的故事，但是与那些幸存者的谈话才是我的旅行中最吸引我的地方。我难以相信他们对我讲述的那些事情。

ＣＮＤ：他们能记住所有的细节？

张纯如：哦，是的，而且我保存了所有的记录。我记日记，而且我对所有和幸存者的面谈以及到那些屠杀现场的参观都进行了摄像。

ＣＮＤ：可见他们很支持你。

张纯如：哦，是的，绝对是这样。他们想要这件事被公诸于众，而且他们的要求非常强烈。

ＣＮＤ：在中国曾经出过几本关于“南京大屠杀”的书，但在我印象中它们都没有成为“畅销书”。

张纯如：实际上，当一本题为《为了不要忘记：一九三七》的书在八十年代的中国出现时，它确实成为了一本畅销书。也许他们没有某种系统（比如畅销书排行榜）去统计数目，但他们说第一天就卖出了大概一万本，然后印刷厂在第一个月内赶印出了十万本。我能想像出那种情况，因为在中国有那么多人，大家都在争先恐后地抢购这本书。这本精装本（《南京浩劫》）将会卖出超过十万本，而它才上市大约两个月。看见这里的人们做着（和中国的人们）一样的事情，很有意思。

ＣＮＤ：你有没有访问“南京大屠杀纪念馆”？

张纯如：我去了，而且他们给了我很多帮助。

ＣＮＤ：那么整个过程中中国政府有没有找你的麻烦？

张纯如：没有。可以说很幸运。我听说其他活动家或摄影师在我之前为同一个目的去过中国，但他们遇到了麻烦。有时被偷偷地录相，跟踪，甚至被驱逐出境。但我都没有遇到这些，一点麻烦也没有。

ＣＮＤ：你是怎么和中国国内的人取得联系的呢？

张纯如：弗利蒙（加州）的吉尔伯特・张告诉我一个中国历史学家的名字，我就传真过去一封信。然后我接到一个中国学者的长途电话，说他们找到了愿意帮助我的人。他说如果我想雇用助手的话，他们也会安排好。一切发生得都很快。当我到达南京大学外国学生住宿中心的时候，他们已经在那里焦急地等着我了。很令人激动。

ＣＮＤ：这本书里突出的一点就是约翰・拉贝的故事。你发现了他的日记……

张纯如：是的，我寻到了保存着这些日记的拉贝后人。我很高兴看到拉贝日记现在已经以德、中和日文发表。原稿被仔细完好地存放在德国官方的资料库中，而“南京大屠杀纪念馆”和耶鲁大学的图书馆都各存一份备份。

ＣＮＤ：你觉得你在南京大屠杀这方面的研究工作做完了吗？

张纯如：还有很多事情可以做。如果我把我所搜集到的每一个故事都写进去，这本书就得写几千页。还有更多的研究可以做，也还有很多文件资料等着被人发现，另外还可以从不同角度写书描写南京大屠杀。我写的这本书有可能成为一本经典之作。史丹福大学心理学系的研究生已经在用它当课本，全美各地的大学也都给我发了Ｅ－ｍａｉｌ。一旦这本书的平装本出版――我听说他们至少要印３０万册――会有更多的大学把它纳入教程。单单利用我手上的资料，我很容易就可以再写出五到十本不同角度的书。但我想转向其它有关第二次世界大战史的话题或是更广泛的题材。

ＣＮＤ：你希望你的这本书能够给其他学者带个头。

张纯如：自然啦。我可能把一些资料捐出去，这样其他学者也可以拿来用。我有关南京大屠杀的资料库存在全美国是最好最完整的之一。如果我想从另一个角度着手的话，我可以写一本，比如说，有关当时傀儡政权的书，或是一本当时南京食物供给情况的书，或是一本日军抢劫掠夺的书……资料真是太多了。但是我觉得写一本综合描述各方面情形的书很重要，而且这本书不只是学术性的，更是面对普通读者的。我希望美国社会的主流都能读到这本书。我这本书的模范实际上是约翰・赫塞（Ｊｏｈｎ　Ｈｅｒｓｅｙ）的《广岛》，一本描述广岛被美国原子弹轰炸的书。那本小册子写得很精彩，不仅在大学里被用来当教材，也被普通大众广泛接受。我当时就想写出这样一本书。不过《南京浩劫》写出来以后，比《广岛》那本书要厚得多。我本来希望这本书能短些，但我想包括的内容实在是太多了。

ＣＮＤ：在写作过程中，你感觉如何？

张纯如：噢，非常沉痛！我感觉如何？我感觉惨不忍睹。一个信念一直伴随着我，支持着我不断工作，那就是我知道人们会来读这本书，这本书会保存在图书馆，让后世的人们都能从中受益。还有，我知道这本书比我本人有更强的生命力，它有可能改变人们的生活，教育别人，而这些人将致力于建设一个新世界，在那里，这样的暴行永远不会再重演。就是这样的想法一直支持着我，可是确实有过那些时刻，我真的觉得难以……

ＣＮＤ：事实上你一直坚持到了最后，真是令人惊异。

张纯如：我就是想，噢，天哪，我真不相信人竟会对自己的同类如此残忍！我简直都不知道应该从何写起――我是说，发生了这么可怕的事情，我该从哪儿着手呢？当时我每天都要读大约五十个这样的事例，阅读本身已经是巨大的刺激。写书从来使精神紧张，可是，这个题目的性质本身已经使写作过程变得困难得多。

ＣＮＤ：你认为我们能够从南京大屠杀吸取什么教训？

张纯如：吸取什么教训？我要说这个事件应当作为一种警戒。我个人以为，在社会和政治环境许可的条件下，所有的人都有实施这种暴行的潜在可能。在阐释这个事件时，我们应该超越日本人对中国人所犯罪行的视角，而把它看作一个启示，看看人能够对其同类做出什么样的事情。我们必须高度警醒，才能理解这样的暴行是如何发生的，并保证权力经由民主制度分解在社会成员中，因为，看来只有民主制度能够防治这种事情的发生。

ＣＮＤ：你的书里有一段引言：“权力即杀戮，绝对权力绝对杀戮。”

张纯如：那是历史学家卢迈尔（Ｒ．Ｊ．Ｒｕｍｍｅｌ）说的。他取阿克唐爵士（Ｌｏａｒｄ　Ａｃｔｏｎ）的著名格言“权力即腐化，绝对权力绝对腐化”，然后把其中的“腐化”改为“杀戮”。我们必须探究政治制度和独裁体制的本质，正是这个本质使得这样的暴行有可能发生。我们应当由此而注意――在任何可能的情况下――权力确实分解于社会成员之中，政府行为确实受到监督和平衡，以保证这样的大屠杀无从重演。

ＣＮＤ：最近日本首相在英国的一家报纸上向英国人民和欧洲人民道歉，可是……

张纯如：我不知道为什么日本至今还没有诚恳地向其在美国和中国的受害者道歉，这是早就应该做的事情了。也许这是因为如果他向美国道歉的话，就会引起极大的骚乱和抗议，特别是那些感到是美国投掷原子弹的牺牲品的日本人。

日本政府声称他们已经向中国致歉，不过我想他们从来没有给过令中国人民感到满意的道歉。但是，只有道歉并不够。日本人应当赔偿他们使欧洲和英国战俘遭受的苦难。一个道歉并不能消除所有的苦难。他们本来应该做得更多。

ＣＮＤ：能谈谈关于中国政府争取日本战争赔偿的努力吗？

张纯如：我所知道的是中国政府实际上阻止了很多人试图索赔的努力。目前，中华人民共和国的政府官员之间并没有协调合作来致力于对日本施压，索取战争赔偿。假使这样的协调努力确实存在，那至少我本人还没有听说过。当然，我希望他们将来会有这样的政策。

ＣＮＤ：你觉得中国人民在战争赔偿问题上可以做什么？他们是不是一定要通过官方渠道？

张纯如：我认为有很多事海外华人都可以做，因为我觉得无论是在中华人民共和国还是中华民国的统治下，人民都可能没有足够的力量争取到任何有保证的战争赔偿。他们的努力很可能被他们的政府所破坏。然而，海外华侨，特别是在加拿大和美国，具有相当可观的财富和力量。就象美国犹太人共同努力迫使德国对欧战受害者给以战争赔偿一样，海外华人可以共同努力迫使日本向大陆的中国人民付出战争赔偿。美籍华人、加籍华人团体正在和美籍日人、加籍日人团体合作，把正义还给受害者。这个运动同时包括了海外韩国人和菲律宾人，我真心希望这个多族裔的合作不断壮大。

你知道李宾斯基法案（Ｌｉｐｉｎｓｋｉ　Ｂｉｌｌ）吗？

ＣＮＤ：知道。〖编注：见本采访结尾的注解。〗

张纯如：我要敦促大家给自己选区的国会议员打电话，要求议员支持这个法案。李宾斯基法案现在有四十多个支持者，但是他们需要更多的人。同时，任何人如果知道有仍然在世的日本战犯在美国或是日本，知道他们的姓名或是目前居所，华人和其他社区团体都应当帮助美国司法部特别调查办公室的埃利・娄森堡（Ｅｌｉ　Ｒｏｓｅｎｂａｕｍ），把有关这些战犯的信息传给他。为了提高对日本侵略罪行的认识，海外华人可以做的另一件事是买一本我的书，捐给当地的社区公共图书馆。要是不想这样做，也可以告诉社区公共图书馆，叫他们直接去买这本书。他们还可以了解当地学校是如何讲授南京大屠杀的，和教师、历史学家、教科书编写者交流有关南京大屠杀的历史。

ＣＮＤ：你认为教育民众是重要的。

张纯如：对，教育是最重要的一步。

ＣＮＤ：还有一个问题：中国人和日本人之间有着如此痛苦的历史记忆，你如何看待他们的关系？有改善的机会吗？

张纯如：如果日本政府毫无保留地道歉，开始战争赔偿，在日本为南京大屠杀的牺牲者修建纪念碑，同时停止对教科书有关南京大屠杀内容的钳制，我想还是有希望的。

ＣＮＤ：正义终究要得到伸张。你对下一本书有计划吗？

张纯如：我有很多主意，但是几个月内恐怕拿不定。

ＣＮＤ：所以你现在就只是在推销这本书？

张纯如：我非常想开始着手写第三本书，但现在的推销活动让我分不了身。

ＣＮＤ：你现在在西雅图，下面你会去哪里？

张纯如：休斯顿和奥斯汀。然后我又会去纽约，三月里去华盛顿ＤＣ的“犹太人大屠杀纪念馆”演讲。我还在很多各种各样的作家会议上演说，例如在加利福尼亚和佛罗里达。我已经美国各地到处奔跑――大概已有２０个城市了吧，而我的巡回访问日程已经安排到六月份，或甚至更远。我实在不知道何时才能结束，而我并不习惯这样的奔波。很高兴有这么多人对这本书感兴趣，但我实在是累坏了。

ＣＮＤ：你希望有更多的休息。

张纯如：根本一点休息都还没有……

ＣＮＤ：非常感谢你接受采访。与你交谈非常愉快！

张纯如：也非常感谢你。我很高兴你会把这些对话写下来。当你把这篇采访通过电脑网络传送出去时，影响一定非常大。

ＣＮＤ：确实如此。祝你一路顺利！

2. Background Information: Iris Chang …………………………….. 26

—————————————————————————-

[CND, 02/23/98] Iris Chang, 29, is a full-time freelance writer living in

northern California with her husband. After receiving her B.S. degree in

Journalism from University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, she worked

briefly as a reporter for the Chicago Tribune and the Associated Press

since 1994. She then won a graduate fellowship to the writing seminars

program at the Johns Hopkins University.

“The Rape of Nanking: The Forgotten Holocaust of World War II” is Chang’s

second book. Her first book, “Thread of the Silkworm”, story of Tsien

Hsue-shen (Qian2 Xue2 Sen1), the “Father of Chinese Missiles”, received

worldwide critical acclaim.

Chang was born to a Chinese family in Princeton, New Jersey (Chinese name:

Zhang1 Chun2 Ru2). Her parents (Shau-Jin Chang and Ying-Ying Chang) moved

to Taiwan from Mainland China in late 1940s and came to U.S. to study as

graduate students at Harvard University. After receiving their PhDs – her

father in physics, her mother in biology – they moved to the University of

Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, where her father works as a tenured physics

professor and mother a microbiologist. It was her parents who first told

Chang about the Nanjing Massacre and how her grandparents narrowly escaped

from it in 1937.

3. A CND Interview with Iris Chang ………………………………. 425

—————————————————————————-

Date:           February 3, 1998

Location:       Alexis Hotel, Seattle, WA, USA

Interviewer:    Jim YU (CND), via telephone

[Editor's note: The interview was conducted in English during one of Iris

Chang's book tours. She began her book tours since November 1997, from

coast to coast, and has covered over 20 cities in the US. The interview

lasted roughly 45 minutes. The following is an edited transcript based on

the recording of the telephone interview. The statements and opinions

expressed in the conversation do not necessarily reflect that of CND's.]

CND:   Iris, thank you for taking the time for this interview. I under-

stand that you will have a book signing in an hour.

Chang: That’s right, but don’t worry about it. Let’s see how far we can go.

CND:   Thank you. How is your book doing now?

Chang: Doing really well. Now it’s #11 on the New York Times bestseller

list on Feb. 8. [It currently stands at #11 on the Feb. 15 list, making

fifth week in a row on the New York Times bestseller list. - ed]

CND:   That’s fantastic.

Chang: Actually it’s #14 this week…

CND:   And it’s going up. How are the book signings going?

Chang: Going really well. I usually see more than 100 people per signing.

CND:   That’s great! Is this kind of reaction what you expected from the

beginning?

Chang: No. I thought the book would receive scholarly acclaim, but not

necessarily become a national best seller.

CND:   What made such an overwhelming reaction from the readers?

Chang: I think the reason the book became a bestseller is that the Chinese

community has waited 60 years for this book. Also, many non-Chinese and

non-Asians are scandalized when they learn about the massacre for the

first time.

CND:   That’s good. At the book signings, are the audience mainly Chinese?

Chang: Not necessarily. Sometimes there are more Caucasians than Asians.

CND:   That’s probably what you look for?

Chang: I want this book to be read by all ethnic groups.

CND:   To your knowledge, is there going to be a Japanese edition of the

book?

Chang: I don’t know yet. I heard that someone wanted to translate it, but

I haven’t heard anything for sure.

CND:   What about other languages?

Chang: Commonwealth Publishing Company (Tian1 Xia4 Wen2 Hua4), a Taipei

based firm, has put out the Chinese edition under my Chinese name, Zhang1

Chun2 Ru2.

CND:   Let’s talk about the research on the book. Why did you want to

write this book?

Chang: I wanted to write it because I wanted to rescue the event from

oblivion. I did not want the event to vanish from history.

CND:   You are saying that at the beginning you didn’t see such a book in

English…

Chang: Right. I learned about the event from my parents. Then in December

1994 I went to a Cupertino conference on the Nanjing Massacre and learned

that there was no English-language book on the subject. I wanted to change

that situation. [The Cupertino, California conference commemorated the

victims of the Nanjing Massacre. It was sponsored by the Global Alliance

for Preserving the History of World War II in Asia. - ed]

CND:   So your research was mainly on raw materials?

Chang: Yes. Primary sources, unpublished materials, documents that were

generated as the event unfolded…I eventually found thousands of pages of

documents in four different languages: Chinese, Japanese, German and

English. Plus information that were never published before, like diaries,

notes, letters, government reports…I also went through Tokyo war crime

trial transcripts.

CND:   Are those materials easy to get access to?

Chang: Not very hard. Of course by now I am pretty expereinced in finding

the materials. For my first book ["Thread of the Silkworm", published by

Basic Books, 1996. A biography of Qian2 Xue2  Sen1, "Father of Chinese

missiles." - ed], it was excruciatingly difficult to find the information.

Compared to that project, this was actually a very easy book to write;

every project seemed simple after that. Also, the massacre happened in the

1930s, and by now a lot of the materials have been archived and de-

classified by the US governemnt, and it was just a lot easier than the

first book, which described events in the 1950s. I found more materials

that I knew what to do with.

CND:   That probably made you even more surprised that there hadn’t been

an English book on it.

Chang: Oh yes, absolutely! But on the other hand, a lot of people don’t

know where to look. I’m a good detective; I know where to find the infor-

mation. For instance, it wasn’t hard for me to find Rabe’s descendents,

even though a lot of other scholars had been looking for him for decades.

CND:   You were probably just the right person to write the book.

Chang: I was persistent. I tried to find every bit of information availa-

ble on the massacre before I even started writing. And I tried to collect

as much information as I could from different views. I felt it was impor-

tant to unearth everything first, digest the material and then construct

the story from the heap of the raw materials.

CND:   So how much material did you collect?

Chang: Thousands of pages…serveral file cabinets.

CND:   That is an amazing amount of work. And you also went to China and

Japan?

Chang: I went to China. I didn’t go to Japan as I mostly corresponded with

the Japanese war veterans. I went to Nanjing.

CND:   When was that?

Chang: The summer of 1995.

CND:   Summer? That was probably not a good time to go. Nanjing is famous

for its summer heat.

Chang: I know!

CND:   Did you visit some victims?

Chang: Yes, and I video taped testimonies of about a dozen victims – in

Taiwan and in Nanjing. I probably videotaped about 10 survivers in Nanjing.

CND:   I see. And did you go alone? All by yourself?

Chang: Yes.

CND:   Growing up in the US, did you feel comfortable travelling in China?

Chang: I felt that I had a mission to complete, so I don’t know if the

words comfortable” or “uncomfortable” should apply.

CND:   You were really focused.

Chang: I was very focused and didn’t have time to think about whether I

was culturally comfortable or not. But I liked the people I met there. I

like the scholars and historians who helped me, and I adore the city of

Nanjing. I did not like the weather – I was often physically ill – but

otherwise I didn’t have any problem. I understood Chinese and I could

carry on a converstaion in Mandarin, so it was actually very enjoyable to

talk to the people and understand how they live. Every scholar I talked to

had a very fascinating story to tell of their own lives, but talking to

the survivors were the most riveting part of my trip. I could not *believe*

the stories they were telling me.

CND:   They remembered all the details?

Chang: Oh yeah, and I kept the records. I kept diaries, and I videotaped

all the interviews with the survivors and my tours to all the execution

sites.

CND:   So they were very supportive.

Chang: Oh yes, absolutely. They wanted that story to be told, and they

were passionate about it.

CND:   There have been a number of Chinese books on Nanjing Massacre, but

in my impression they didn’t become “bestsellers” in China.

Chang: Actually, when a book entitled “Lest We Forget: 1937″ came out in

China in the 1980s, it did become a bestseller. Maybe they don’t  have a

system [like bestseller lists] counting the numbers, but they said that

something like 10,000 copies of the book were sold out on the first day,

then 100,000 copies were rushed out from the printing shops in the first

month. I can imagine that because there are so many people in China, they

were snatching copies of the book as quickly as they could. This hardcover

book ["The Rape of Nanking"] will sell more than 100,000, and it’s been

out for only about two months. It’s interesting to see people doing the

same thing [as in China] here.

CND:   Did you visit the Nanjing Massacre Museum?

Chang: Yes, I did. They were very helpful too.

CND:   Did you encounter any problem with the Chinese government?

Chang: No, I was very fortunate. There were other activists or filmmakers

who had gone to China before me and encountered problems. They were

secretly videotaped, followed, or even kicked out of the country. That

didn’t happen to me. There was not one single problem.

CND:   How did you get the contact there?

Chang: Gilbert Chang in Fremont gave me the name of a historian in China,

and I faxed over a letter. Then I got a long-distance phone call from a

scholar in China. He said that they found people to help me, and if I

wanted to hire assistants, they would line that up too. It happened very

quickly. When I got to Nanjing University international student housing

center, they were already there waiting for me and eager to meet me. It

was all very exciting.

CND:   One special aspect of this book is the story of John Rabe. You

discovered the diary…

Chang: Yes, I tracked down the descendents who kept his diaries and I am

very happy now that the diary is published in German, Chinese and Japanese,

and the original is well preserved in official Germany archives. There is

also a copy in Nanjing Massacre Museum, and a copy in the Yale Divinity

School Library.

CND:   Do you feel that your research on this topic is completed?

Chang: There is more that can be done. If I had put in every story that I

read from my files, it would have been thousands of pages long. More

research could be done, and there are still more documents waiting to be

discovered. And there can be other books to be written to attack different

aspects of the Nanjing Massacre. I’ve written this one book which may

become a classic. It’s been already taught in Stanford Universtiy psycho-

logy graduate class, and I’ve been getting e-mails from colleges all over

the country. Once the paperback is published by Viking Penguin – I heard

rumors that they were going to print at least 300,000 copies – more univer-

sities may adopt of this book for their curriculums.

Just from all the files I have, I could easily write 5 or 10 scholarly

books from different angles based on the material. But I’d rather move on

to other events in World War II history, or perhaps other topics in

general.

CND:   You hope that your book will make other scholars to follow.

Chang: Sure. I may donate some of the material to archives for other

scholars to use it. I have one of the best collections of materials on the

Nanjing Massacre in this country. If I wanted to focus on different aspect,

I could write, say, a book on the puppet government, a book on the food

situation in Nanjing, or a book on the looting by the Japanese troops…

there is just so much material. But I felt it was more important to write

a book that combines all these different issues for an audience that is

not only scholarly but general. I want mainsteam America to read this book.

The model for my book is actually John Hersey’s classic “Hiroshima”

[published by Knopf, 1985], which described the atomic bombing of Hiro-

shima. It was a little gem of a book taught in college classes but also

popular among general audiences. That’s the book I wanted to write, but

“The Rape of Nanking” turned out to be thicker than “Hiroshima.” I wanted

my book to be shorter, but there was so much information I wanted to

include.

CND:   When you wrote the book, how did you feel?

Chang: Oh, agonizing! How did I feel? I felt miserable. One thing that

just stayed with me and kept me going was the knowledge that people would

go out to read this book, and that it was going to be preserved in lib-

raries, so future generations could learn from it. Also, the knowledge

that this book would outlive me and perhaps change lives and educate

others who would work towards building a new world in which this kind of

atrocity wouldn’t happen again. That was what stayed with me, but there

were times when I really had a difficult time…

CND:   It’s amazing that you actually managed to finish the book.

Chang: I just thought, God, I can’t believe people can be so cruel to each

other! I didn’t even know where to start – I mean, this horrible thing

happened, so where do I even begin? I was reading about 50 of such stories

each day, and it was just overwhelming. It’s always stressful writing a

book, but then the nature of the topic made it even more difficult.

CND:   What lessons do you think we can learn from this massacre?

Chang: Lessons to be learned? I would say that this event should be viewed

as a cautionary tale. I personally think that all people are capable com-

mitting of this kind of atrocities, if they are put under the right social

and political circumstances. We should see it as a story that transcends

what the Japanese did to the Chinese and a metaphor of what a human being

can do to another human being. We have to be vigilant to understand how

these atrocities happened and make sure that power is defused among people

in a democratic system, because it seems that only democracy prevents

these kind of things from happening.

CND:   There is a quote in your book, “Power kills. Absolute power kills

absolutely.”

Chang: That’s what the historian R. J. Rummel said. He took the famous

Lord Acton quote, “Power corrupts, and absolute power corrupts absolutely”

and changed the word “corrupt” to “kill.” We have to probe the nature of

political systems and dictatorships that make it possible for such atro-

cities to occur. We should see to it that – whenever possible – power is

defused among people and that there are checks and balances in government

so that this kind of massacre doesn’t happen again.

CND:   Recently in a British newspaper, the Japanese Prime Minister

apologized to the British people and the European people, but…

Chang: I don’t know why Japan hasn’t yet given out a sincere apology to

their American and Chinese victims, which is long overdue. Perhaps it is

because if he apologized to America, that may result in a huge uproar and

outcry from those Japanese who feel victimized by the US dropping of the

atomic bombs.

The Japanese government has claimed that they have already apologzied to

China, but I don’t think they have ever given out an apology that the

Chinese people have found satisfactory.

But apologies are not enough. The Japanese should pay reparation for the

suffering they inflicted upon the European and English POWs. An apology

doesn’t erase all the suffering. They’ve got to do better than that.

CND:   What do you know about Chinese government’s effort to get the war

compensation from Japan?

Chang: All I know is that the Chinese government has actually impeded the

effort of a lot of people who have tried. At the moment, there is no

concerted effort among PRC officials to pressure Japan to pay reparations.

If there is, I am not aware of it yet. I certainly hope they do have such

a policy in the future.

CND:   What do you think the Chinese people can do about the war compen-

sation, or do they have to go through the government?

Chang: I think there is a lot the overseas Chinese can accomplish, because

I think the people under the PRC and ROC regimes may not have the power to

achieve anything in the way of securing reparations. Their effort might be

destroyed by their governments. But, overseas Chinese, especially in

Canada and the United States, have considerable wealth and power. Just as

the American Jews worked together to pressure Germany into paying repara-

tions to victims in Europe, the overseas Chinese people can work together

to pressure Japan to pay compensation to the Chinese people in the main-

land. Chinese American and Chinese Canadian groups are working together

with Japanese American and Japanese Canadian groups to bring justice to

the victims. The movement includes overseas Koreans and Filipinos as well,

and I really hope this multi-ethnic effort grows.

You know about the Lipinski Bill, right?

CND:   Yes. [See notes attached at the end of the conversation - ed.]

Chang: I would urge people to call up their local congressman or congress-

woman to ask him or her to support the bill. The Lipinski bill has more

than 40 supporters but they need more. And if anyone knows of the names

and whereabouts of Japanese war criminals who are still alive in this

country or in Japan, Chinese and other groups should help Eli Rosenbaum

of the Office of Special Investigations of the US Department of Justice

by supplying him with important information on these men. Another thing

that American Chinese can do to promote the awareness of the Japanese war

crime is to buy a copy of my book and donate it to local public library.

Or if they don’t want to do that, they can go to the local public library

to tell them to buy the book. They could also find out how Rape of Nanking

is being taught in local schools, and talk to teachers, historians and

textbook writers about the history of the Nanjing Massacre.

CND:   You see that it’s important to keep people educated.

Chang: Yes, education is the best step.

CND:   One more question: How do you see the relationship between the

Chinese and the Japanese with such a painful history in memory? Is there

any chance for improvement?

Chang: If the Japanese government apologize profusely, start to pay

reparations, build monuments in Japan to commemorate the victims of

Nanjing Massacre, and also stop censoring their textbook on the Rape of

Nanking, I think there is some hope.

CND:   Justice has to be served. Do you have your next project in mind?

Chang: I have a lot of different ideas, but I won’t be certain for the

next few months.

CND:   So your current plan is just to promote the book?

Chang: I really would like to start writing a third book immediately, but

I am too busy promoting this book right now.

CND:   You are in Seattle now, and what are your next stops?

Chang: Houston and Austin. Then I have to go to New York again, and in

March I have to go to Washington D.C. to speak at the Holocaust Memorial

Museum. [For details, refer to the "Update" column in this issue - ed.]

I also have a lot of different writers conferences to speak at, such as

in Santa Barbara, CA, and Palm Beach, Florida. I really have been all over

the country – maybe 20 cities already, and I am booked up to visit more

cities through June or maybe even later. I don’t really see any end in

sight. I am not used to all this traveling. I’m glad there is so much

interest, but I am also exhausted.

CND:   You want to have more breaks.

Chang: I don’t have any breaks…

CND:   Thank you very much. It’s a great pleasure talking to you.

Chang: Thank you so much. I am so happy that you are writing this. This

article will have huge impact once it goes out electronically.

CND:   Yes indeed. Good luck on your book tours.
